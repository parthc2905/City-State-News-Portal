The CIVIX Journalism Standards

At CIVIX, we're committed to ethical, accurate, and impactful hyper-local journalism. Every article must meet these core principles.

Core Principles

Our journalism is guided by three fundamental values:

✓ Accuracy First
Every fact must be verified. Every quote must be accurate. Every source must be credible. If you're not 100% certain, don't publish it.
🎯 Local Impact
Focus on stories that directly affect your city's residents. How does this news change their daily lives? Why should they care?
⚖️ Fair & Balanced
Present multiple perspectives. Give all relevant parties a chance to respond. Avoid bias and let readers form their own opinions.
2

Writing Clear Headlines

Your headline is the first (and sometimes only) thing readers see. Make it count.

Headline Best Practices

✓ DO
  • Be specific and descriptive
  • Use active voice
  • Include key facts
  • Keep it under 70 characters
  • Answer: Who, What, Where, When
✕ DON'T
  • Use clickbait or sensationalism
  • Include opinion in headlines
  • Use ALL CAPS
  • Make promises you can't deliver
  • Use jargon or acronyms
✓ Good Example:
"Mumbai Metro Line 3 Opens March 15, Cutting Commute Times by 40 Minutes"
✕ Bad Example:
"You Won't Believe What's Happening to Mumbai Traffic!"
3

Structure Your Article

Follow the inverted pyramid: most important information first, supporting details after.

The Perfect Article Structure

1. Lead Paragraph (First 2-3 sentences)
Answer the 5 W's: Who, What, Where, When, Why (and How). This should be a complete summary of the story.

2. Supporting Details (Next 2-4 paragraphs)
Expand on the lead with crucial facts, quotes, and context. Include the most newsworthy information here.

3. Background Information (Middle section)
Provide context: history, related events, why this matters now. Help readers understand the bigger picture.

4. Additional Details (Later paragraphs)
Include secondary quotes, statistics, expert opinions, and less critical information.

5. Conclusion (Final paragraph)
End with forward-looking information: what happens next, future implications, or a powerful closing quote.

💡 Pro Tip: The Coffee Test
If a reader spills coffee on your article after the first paragraph, they should still understand the complete story. That's how strong your lead should be.
4

Fact-Checking & Sources

Accuracy is non-negotiable. Every fact must be verified, every source must be credible.

Source Requirements

Minimum Requirements:

  • Breaking news: At least 2 independent sources
  • Investigative pieces: 3+ sources, with documentation
  • Opinion pieces: Clearly labeled as opinion
  • Statistics: Link to original data source

Credible Sources

✓ Reliable Sources
  • Government officials (named)
  • Subject matter experts
  • Official documents
  • Eyewitnesses (verified)
  • Academic research
  • Press releases (verified)
✕ Questionable Sources
  • Anonymous social media posts
  • Unverified rumors
  • Biased advocacy groups
  • Wikipedia (use as starting point only)
  • Screenshots without verification
  • "People say..." or "Many believe..."
✓ Fact-Checking Checklist
  • All names spelled correctly
  • All titles and positions accurate
  • All dates and times verified
  • All statistics from original sources
  • All quotes accurate and in context
  • All locations correct
  • All phone numbers and websites working
5

Style & Tone Guidelines

Write clearly, concisely, and objectively. Your job is to inform, not to impress.

Writing Style Rules

Voice & Tense:

  • Use active voice: "The mayor announced" not "It was announced by the mayor"
  • Write in past tense for news events
  • Use present tense only for general truths or ongoing situations

Sentence Structure:

  • One idea per sentence
  • Average 15-20 words per sentence
  • Vary sentence length for readability
  • Avoid complex compound sentences

Word Choice:

  • Use simple, concrete words
  • Avoid jargon and technical terms (or explain them)
  • Be specific: "increased by 15%" not "increased significantly"
  • Avoid clichés and idioms
📝 The 8th-Grade Rule
Write for an 8th-grade reading level. This doesn't mean dumbing down—it means clear, accessible writing that anyone can understand.
6

Before You Publish

Use this final checklist before submitting your article for review.

✓ Pre-Publication Checklist
  • Headline is clear, accurate, under 70 characters
  • Lead paragraph answers 5 W's
  • All facts verified with sources
  • At least 2 independent sources cited
  • All quotes accurate and attributed
  • Article is balanced and fair
  • Grammar and spelling checked
  • No personal opinions included
  • Featured image added (1200x630px)
  • Proper category and tags selected
  • Excerpt written (2-3 sentences)
  • Read aloud for flow and clarity
🎯 Final Tip: Sleep On It
If possible, write your article, save it as a draft, and review it the next day with fresh eyes. You'll catch errors and improve clarity.
